# Build What Pays — Manuscript Ruleset

## Recommendation

Treat *Build What Pays* as a **premium field manual / authority asset**, not a padded traditional business book.

That means:
- no vanity length
- no filler bridges just to increase page count
- no generic case-study sludge
- every chapter must earn its place
- the design must serve the manuscript, not hide weak content

## Asset Concept

**Reader**
Solo builders, technical founders, operators, and product-minded independents trying to pick stronger ideas and avoid wasting time on weak bets.

**Problem**
They have too many plausible ideas, too little judgment, and no hard framework for deciding what deserves the next 30 days.

**Promise**
Give the reader a practical decision system for finding, scoring, validating, and narrowing ideas into wedges that can pay.

**Angle**
This is not inspiration, startup theater, or generic “AI ideas” content. It is an operator’s field manual: filtering, scoring, killing, and validating.

**Format**
Short premium field manual with diagrams, scorecards, and worksheets.

**Distribution**
Primary: direct PDF / Gumroad-style premium asset.
Secondary: authority-building ebook / downloadable lead asset / consulting credibility piece.

**CTA**
Reader finishes with one opportunity narrowed, scored, and either advanced or killed.

## Positioning Rule

This manuscript should feel:
- more practical than a blog post
- more concentrated than a normal business book
- more opinionated than a generic AI summary
- more reusable than a long essay
- more like a toolkit than a memoir

## Length Rule

Target:
- **Core manuscript target:** 4,800–6,500 words of real prose
- **Plus diagrams / tools / worksheets:** enough to justify premium utility without prose padding

Current reference points:
- `build-what-pays-full.typ`: ~4,812 words
- `build-what-pays-final-draft.md`: ~4,581 words
- current `build-what-pays-marginalia` manuscript text is materially thinner than source and needs parity restoration before any further design judgment

Implication:
- do **not** chase a 20k+ or 40k-word book unless the user explicitly changes direction
- do restore all missing utility sections and chapter support text so the manuscript is complete at its intended density

## Non-Negotiables

1. Structure first
2. Chapter purpose before prose
3. Source manuscript parity before visual polish
4. No chapter may exist only to carry a diagram
5. Every appendix/tool page must do real work
6. Every diagram must either:
   - clarify a framework, or
   - increase usability / retention / product value
7. If a page looks good but weakens clarity, it loses

## Style Rules

### Voice
- direct
- practical
- operator-minded
- anti-fluff
- opinionated but not performative
- explanatory only where needed

### Paragraph Rule
Each paragraph must do one of four things:
1. sharpen the framework
2. increase clarity
3. prove a point
4. improve usability

If it does none of those, cut it.

### Example Rule
Prefer:
- one strong example
- one concrete operator scenario
- one believable wedge

Avoid:
- three weak examples saying the same thing
- generic startup folklore
- broad motivational filler

### Diagram Rule
Diagrams are not decoration.
Each one must:
- make the framework easier to grasp, or
- make the asset feel more usable as a product

### Page System Rule
Use two modes only:
1. **Reading pages** — quiet prose, clean hierarchy, optional sparse margin notes
2. **Graphic spreads** — hero framework diagrams, scorecards, worksheets, chapter opener statements

Do not force hero diagrams into ordinary prose-page behavior.
